Rise of Nationalism in Europe

From the fragmented feudal kingdoms of 1789 to the unified nation-states of the 19th century — the story of how a continent was redrawn by ideas, revolutions, and bloodshed.

Section 01 — Setting the Stage

The Romantic Vision of a Nation

Before the 19th century, Europe was a patchwork of kingdoms, empires, and duchies — not nation-states. The concept of a unified people sharing a common language, culture, and destiny was largely an idea in the minds of artists, poets, and philosophers.

Frédéric Sorrieu's Vision (1848)

In his famous lithograph series "The Dream of Worldwide Democratic and Social Republics", French artist Frédéric Sorrieu visualised a world made up of democratic and social republics — nations marching in a procession past the Statue of Liberty, bearing their own flags and national symbols. The United States led the procession, followed by France. The shattered remains of crowns and sceptres lay scattered on the ground — symbols of the old order being crushed.

What is a Nation-State?

A nation-state is a political entity where the cultural and geographic territory of a nation aligns with the political boundaries of a state. People of that nation believe they share a common past, language, territory, and destiny — and this shared identity becomes the basis for political legitimacy.

🏛

Absolutist Monarchies (Before 1789)

Kings ruled on the basis of divine right. There was no concept of "the people" as a political community. Subjects owed loyalty to the crown, not to a nation.

💡

The Enlightenment's Role

18th-century philosophers like Rousseau promoted ideas of popular sovereignty — that political authority derives from "the people." This planted seeds of nationalist thought.

🗺

Europe in 1815

The Congress of Vienna (1815) tried to restore the old conservative order after Napoleon's defeat. Metternich called nationalism a "disease" threatening stability.

"When France sneezes, the rest of Europe catches cold." — Duke Metternich, Austrian Chancellor, on the spread of revolutionary ideas
Section 02 — The Making of Nationalism

Culture, Language & Nationalism

Nationalism was not just a political movement — it was deeply cultural. Literature, folk tales, music, and language were the tools through which people imagined themselves as a single community.

The Role of Culture in Building Nations

Key Concept — Romanticism

Romantic nationalism emerged as a cultural movement in the early 19th century. Romantics emphasised emotion, nature, and folk traditions over Enlightenment reason. They believed national spirit (Volksgeist) could be recovered through myths, poetry, and music.

Johann Gottfried Herder (German philosopher) argued that true German culture was to be found among the common people (das Volk) — their folk songs, poetry, and dances, not in the courts of kings.

Grimm Brothers & Folklore

Brothers Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm collected German folk tales in the early 1800s. Their work was explicitly nationalist — to define a uniquely German cultural identity through shared stories, myths, and language. This was not unique to Germany; across Europe, similar efforts were underway to document and revive local languages.

Language and Nationalism

Language was a powerful unifying force. The Polish language was suppressed under Russian occupation — Russian was imposed as the official language. Similarly, in Czech territories under Habsburg rule, German dominated. The revival of local languages became an act of political resistance.

🎵

Music as a Weapon

Karol Kurpinski in Poland celebrated the national struggle through operas and music, turning Polish folk themes into powerful nationalist symbols during the 1830-31 revolt.

📖

The Print Revolution

Books, pamphlets, and newspapers spread nationalist ideas rapidly. Vernacular press (in local languages rather than Latin) reached the common people, creating shared political consciousness.

🎨

Visual Arts

Artists like Eugène Delacroix painted dramatic scenes of liberty and revolution. Germania (a female allegory of Germany) became a popular nationalist symbol in 19th-century art.

Allegory and National Symbols

Nations were often personified as female allegorical figures. In France, Marianne became the symbol of liberty and the Republic. In Germany, Germania wore a crown of oak leaves — a symbol of German heroism. These symbols appeared on coins, stamps, statues, and official seals to foster national identity.

🔑
CBSE Exam Tip: Be ready to distinguish between Marianne (France) and Germania (Germany) as national symbols. Know the objects each carries and what they symbolise.
Section 03 — The French Revolution & Napoleon

The French Revolution and Its Legacy

The French Revolution of 1789 was the first major political upheaval that demonstrated the power of ordinary people to overthrow a monarchy and reshape a state. It became the blueprint for nationalist movements across Europe.

How the Revolution Created Nationalism

Before 1789, the "nation" was merely the king's domain. The revolutionaries turned this upside down — sovereignty was transferred from the monarch to the citizens of France. Key acts:

Revolutionary Measures (1789–1799)

• A centralised administrative system replaced the patchwork of regional powers.

• A unified legal code — the Metric system and common customs — replaced regional variations.

• The French language (Parisian dialect) was promoted as the language of the nation, suppressing regional dialects like Breton, Basque, and Alsatian.

• New symbols: the tricolour flag, La Marseillaise (national anthem), oaths of loyalty to the nation.

• The Estates General was reconstituted as the National Assembly — representing the nation, not estates.

Napoleon Bonaparte and the Spread of Ideas

Napoleon (ruled 1799–1815) exported French revolutionary ideas across Europe through conquest. Though he was an emperor, not a democrat, his Napoleonic Code (1804) dismantled feudal privileges and introduced:

⚖️

Equality Before the Law

Abolished hereditary privileges of the nobility. All male citizens were legally equal — a radical concept in feudal Europe.

🏦

Property Rights

Secured the right to private property, ending arbitrary royal confiscation. Peasants were freed from feudal dues in French-occupied territories.

🗳

Uniform Administration

Replaced dozens of local legal systems with a single, rational legal code. Created efficient, centralised bureaucracies modelled on the French state.

🏫

Abolition of Guilds

Dismantled the guild system that restricted trade. This promoted economic liberalism and eventually connected to the rise of a nationalist middle class.

The Paradox: Liberation or Colonisation?

Napoleon's armies were initially welcomed in many European cities — they came as liberators from feudalism. But the reality of French occupation — heavy taxation, forced conscription, censorship in the interest of French imperialism — soon turned local populations against them.

This paradox created something Napoleon never intended: by awakening people to the idea of national sovereignty while simultaneously oppressing them as foreigners, he accidentally fuelled nationalist revolts across Europe.

"My true glory is not to have won forty battles... Waterloo will erase the memory of so many victories. But what nothing will destroy... is my Civil Code." — Napoleon Bonaparte
Section 04 — The Age of Revolutions

Revolutions of 1830 and 1848

The decades between 1820 and 1850 saw a series of nationalist uprisings across Europe — the "Springtime of Nations." While most ultimately failed, they planted seeds that would bear fruit by 1871.

1821

Greek War of Independence

Greece revolted against the Ottoman Empire. The Greek cause attracted enormous European sympathy — the poet Lord Byron died fighting for Greek independence. Greece gained independence in 1832, the first new nation-state formed through nationalist struggle.

1830

The July Revolution & Belgian Independence

Revolution in France overthrew Charles X. Belgium broke away from the Netherlands and declared independence. These events triggered revolutions across Germany, Poland, and Italy — all suppressed.

1848

"The Springtime of Nations"

Revolutionary uprisings exploded across France, Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy, and Poland simultaneously. In France, a republic was proclaimed. In Germany, the Frankfurt Parliament met — dominated by the middle class, it tried to draft a liberal constitution for a unified German nation.

1848

Frankfurt Parliament — Rise and Fall

The Frankfurt Parliament offered the crown of a united Germany to the Prussian king Friedrich Wilhelm IV. He rejected it, calling it a "crown from the gutter" — a crown offered by revolutionaries had no legitimacy in his eyes. The Parliament collapsed. Lesson: liberal nationalism could not succeed without military force.

1848

The Liberal Revolution — Social Fault Lines

Within the revolutionary movement, tensions emerged. The liberal middle class (bourgeoisie) wanted constitutional government and national unity. The working class and peasants demanded social and economic rights. When threatened by radical demands, the liberals chose order over revolution — and sided with the conservatives.

NCERT Focus — The Conservative Reaction

After the failure of the 1848 revolutions, European conservatives briefly reasserted control. The Austrian Empire under Metternich, the Prussian Junkers, and Russian Tsar all cracked down on nationalist movements. However, they could not erase the ideas — nationalism would return, this time led not by liberals but by conservative realpolitik politicians like Bismarck.

Section 05 — Unification

Germany and Italy Unified

The unification of Germany and Italy in the 1860s-70s was achieved not through popular revolution but through war and skilled political manipulation — proving that nationalism could be a tool of conservative statecraft as much as liberal idealism.

Unification of Germany — Bismarck's Blood and Iron

Otto von Bismarck (1815–1898), the Prussian Chancellor, is the central figure of German unification. He famously declared in 1862 that the great questions of the day would be settled not by speeches and majority decisions, but by "iron and blood" — meaning military force and industrial power.

⚔️

Three Wars of Unification

1864: Prussia + Austria vs Denmark (won Schleswig-Holstein). 1866: Austro-Prussian War — Prussia defeated Austria, excluded it from German affairs. 1870–71: Franco-Prussian War — the decisive victory that cemented German unity.

👑

The German Empire Proclaimed (1871)

On 18 January 1871, at the Palace of Versailles (in humiliated France!), the Prussian king Wilhelm I was proclaimed German Emperor. Germany was now the most powerful state in continental Europe.

Unification of Italy — Mazzini, Cavour, Garibaldi

Italy's unification (Risorgimento — "resurgence") had three great architects, each representing a different strand of nationalism:

FigureRoleMethodKey Contribution
Giuseppe Mazzini (1805–1872) Idealist visionary Revolutionary agitation, propaganda Founded Young Italy (1831) — a secret society for Italian unification. Inspired a generation with the idea of a unified Italian republic. Most revolts he inspired failed, but the idea survived.
Count Camillo di Cavour (1810–1861) Shrewd statesman Diplomacy and war As Prime Minister of Sardinia-Piedmont, he allied with France against Austria (1859). Used diplomacy — not popular revolution — to expand Piedmontese territory and create conditions for unification.
Giuseppe Garibaldi (1807–1882) Military hero Armed guerrilla struggle Led the famous Expedition of the Thousand (1860) — 1,000 red-shirted volunteers (Redshirts) captured Sicily and Naples from the Bourbon kings. Handed the south to King Victor Emmanuel II.
Key Fact — Italy Unified (1861)

In 1861, Victor Emmanuel II of Sardinia-Piedmont was proclaimed King of Italy. However, the unification was not complete — Rome was still under papal control (French troops protected the Pope) and Venice was under Austria. These were gained later: Venice in 1866, Rome in 1870.

Why Nationalism Succeeded Now — and Not in 1848?

The key difference was realpolitik (practical politics) over idealism. Bismarck and Cavour understood that in the post-1848 world, nation-states would be built by military power, diplomatic alliances, and economic strength — not by liberal constitutions and popular assemblies.

⚠️
Common CBSE Error: Students confuse Garibaldi's "Expedition of the Thousand" (1860) with the 1848 revolutions. They are separate events. Garibaldi operated in 1860, after the failed revolutions.
Section 06 — The Dark Side

Nationalism & Imperialism

By the late 19th century, nationalism had transformed from a liberating force into a dangerous one. What began as a movement for freedom became a justification for domination — of other nations and peoples.

The Balkans — Europe's Powder Keg

The Balkan region (southeastern Europe — modern-day Serbia, Bosnia, Croatia, Romania, Bulgaria, Greece, Albania) was the most explosive site of nationalist tension by 1900. The declining Ottoman Empire had lost control over much of the region, and various ethnic groups — Slavs, Greeks, Bulgarians, Serbs — were competing for independence and territory.

🧨

Pan-Slavism

Russia promoted the idea of Slavic solidarity (Pan-Slavism) to justify interference in Ottoman and Habsburg territories. Russia positioned itself as the protector of Slavic peoples — a cover for imperial expansion.

🏰

Ottoman Decline

As the "sick man of Europe" weakened, newly independent Balkan nations fought each other for the scraps of Ottoman territory — the Balkan Wars of 1912–13 redrew the map yet again.

💥

Road to World War I

The assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand (heir to the Habsburg throne) by Gavrilo Princip — a Bosnian Serb nationalist — on 28 June 1914 in Sarajevo set off a chain reaction that exploded into World War I.

Nationalism as a Justification for Imperialism

European powers used nationalism to justify their colonial empires. "Civilising the inferior races" was framed as a national duty. The British called it the "White Man's Burden." The French called it the mission civilisatrice. In reality, it was economic exploitation wrapped in nationalist rhetoric.

Within Europe, nationalism also fuelled ethnic tensions. The Habsburg Empire, which ruled over Germans, Czechs, Poles, Hungarians, Italians, Slovaks, Croats, Serbs, and others, was increasingly unstable as each group demanded its own nation-state. The empire's policy of imposing German culture on non-German populations created constant resentment.

"Nationalism is not to be confused with love of country — which is something entirely different. Nationalism is the love of a country at the expense of someone else's country." — Inspired by contemporary European political thought, late 19th century
1871

Germany and Italy Unified

The map of Europe is redrawn. Two new great powers emerge. The conservative order of 1815 is dead.

1878

Congress of Berlin

Balkans parcelled out among great powers. Serbia, Romania, Montenegro gain independence. But the region remains volatile.

1912–13

Balkan Wars

Two successive wars in the Balkans. Serbia doubles its territory. Austria-Hungary is alarmed. Tensions reach breaking point.

1914

The Shot That Started a World War

Assassination of Franz Ferdinand in Sarajevo. Nationalist tensions that had been building for decades explode into the Great War — 20 million dead.

Section 07 — Vocabulary

Key Terms & Concepts

Master these terms for full marks in short-answer and definition questions. These appear repeatedly in CBSE board exams.

Nationalism The belief that people sharing a common language, culture, territory, and history constitute a nation with the right to self-governance.
Nation-State A political unit where the cultural boundaries of a nation align with the political boundaries of a state. E.g., France after the Revolution.
Romanticism A cultural movement stressing emotion, folk traditions, and national spirit (Volksgeist) as the basis of national identity. Contrasted with Enlightenment rationalism.
Liberalism Belief in individual freedoms, constitutional government, and free markets. In 19th-century Europe, liberals typically supported national unification and constitutional monarchies.
Conservatism Defence of established institutions — monarchy, church, and aristocracy. Opposed rapid change. Metternich was the great conservative statesman of the post-1815 era.
Realpolitik Practical politics based on power and self-interest rather than ideals. Associated with Bismarck — who used war and diplomacy to unify Germany.
Zollverein A customs union established by Prussia in 1834. Removed trade barriers among 39 German states, creating economic unity that preceded political unification.
Plebiscite A direct vote by the people on a specific question, such as whether a region should join a particular state. Used extensively during Italian unification.
Allegory A visual or literary representation of an abstract idea. Nations were often represented as female allegorical figures — Marianne (France), Germania (Germany).
Risorgimento Italian for "resurgence" — the political and cultural movement for the unification of Italy in the 19th century, associated with Mazzini, Cavour, and Garibaldi.
Pan-Slavism Movement advocating political and cultural unity of all Slavic peoples. Russia used Pan-Slavism to justify interference in Balkan affairs and opposition to Austria-Hungary.
Balkanisation The fragmentation of a region into small, hostile states. The Balkans gave their name to this process as the Ottoman Empire collapsed into competing ethnic mini-states.
Napoleonic Code Legal reforms introduced by Napoleon (1804) ensuring equality before law, property rights, and secular administration. Spread French revolutionary ideas across Europe.
Young Italy A secret revolutionary society founded by Mazzini in 1831, dedicated to Italian unification through republican revolution. Inspired youth-led nationalist movements.
Frankfurt Parliament An elected assembly of German liberals (1848) that attempted to draft a unified German constitution. Collapsed when Prussia rejected the crown offered to its king.
Congress of Vienna 1815 conference that reorganised Europe after Napoleon's defeat. Led by Metternich, it aimed to restore conservative monarchies and suppress nationalism.

Section 08 — Board Exam Preparation

Exam-Ready Questions & Answers

These questions are framed on CBSE board exam patterns — 1-mark, 3-mark, and 5-mark questions that have appeared in previous years or are highly probable.

1-Mark Questions (Define / Identify)

Q. What was the Zollverein?
A Prussian-led customs union (1834) that abolished tariff barriers among 39 German states and created a unified economic territory, laying the groundwork for political unification.
Q. Who designed the vision of "Worldwide Democratic Republics" in 1848?
French artist Frédéric Sorrieu, in his lithograph series depicting nations marching past the Statue of Liberty.
Q. What was the "Expedition of the Thousand"?
Garibaldi's 1860 campaign with 1,000 volunteers (Redshirts) to capture Sicily and Naples from the Bourbon king, handing southern Italy to King Victor Emmanuel II.
Q. Name the female allegory of France and Germany.
France = Marianne; Germany = Germania.

3-Mark Questions (Short Answer)

Q. Explain how the French Revolution contributed to the growth of nationalism in Europe.
The Revolution transferred sovereignty from the king to the people (popular sovereignty). It created a unified legal system, national symbols (tricolour, anthem), and promoted the idea that citizens owe loyalty to the nation. Napoleon spread these ideas through conquest, dismantling feudal systems across Europe via the Napoleonic Code.
Q. Compare the roles of Mazzini, Cavour, and Garibaldi in Italian unification.
Mazzini = idealist visionary who inspired the movement through Young Italy and the concept of a republic. Cavour = pragmatic statesman who used diplomacy and war (alliance with France against Austria) to expand Piedmont. Garibaldi = military hero whose Redshirts captured southern Italy through armed struggle, then handed it to the crown.
Q. Why did the 1848 revolutions fail?
Three main reasons: (1) Liberal middle class sided with conservatives when the working class demanded economic rights, splitting the revolutionary coalition. (2) Conservative powers (Austria, Russia, Prussia) suppressed revolts militarily. (3) Nationalist movements lacked military backing — the Frankfurt Parliament's offer of a crown was rejected by the Prussian king who refused to accept legitimacy from "the gutter."

5-Mark Questions (Long Answer)

Q. "Nationalism in the 19th century changed from a force of liberation to one of oppression." Discuss with examples.
Initially, nationalism was a liberating force: it overthrew absolute monarchies (French Revolution), ended foreign domination (Greek independence, Italian unification), and promoted popular sovereignty. However, by the late 19th century it became oppressive: (a) European powers used nationalist rhetoric to justify colonial imperialism in Africa and Asia. (b) Majority nationalist groups in multi-ethnic empires (e.g., German nationalism in the Habsburg Empire) suppressed minority cultures and languages. (c) Pan-Slavism was used by Russia to justify imperial expansion. (d) Balkan nationalism created intense rivalries that eventually triggered World War I, killing 20 million. The same idea that once fought for freedom became a tool for domination.
Q. Describe Bismarck's role in German unification. How was it different from the liberal nationalist approach of 1848?
Bismarck (Prussian Chancellor from 1862) used "blood and iron" — military force and political manipulation. He fought three wars: vs Denmark (1864), vs Austria (1866), and vs France (1870-71). Each war expanded Prussian territory and weakened rivals. The German Empire was proclaimed at Versailles in 1871. This contrasted sharply with 1848: the Frankfurt Parliament tried to achieve unity through democratic elections and constitutional debate. The middle-class liberals failed because they had no military force and no willingness to mobilise the working classes. Bismarck succeeded because he harnessed Prussian military power and manipulated nationalist sentiment from above, making nationalism a conservative tool rather than a liberal one.
